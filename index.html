A note before you begin

In this curriculum we're discussing nuclear weapons, current military practices, and active conflicts — not as distant history, but as systems that are operating right now. Some of this material may feel heavy or unsettling. That's normal.

We're not here to overwhelm you or leave you feeling helpless. We're here because we take you seriously — seriously enough to share real information, and seriously enough to believe you can engage with complexity and find your own way to respond.

What we're asking

Stay curious. Engage honestly with the material. Ask hard questions.

What we're offering

Evidence-based information, space to think critically, and pathways from understanding to action.

Key concepts

Nuclear deterrence

The logic that possessing nukes prevents use — and the systems failures that challenge it.

Risk by accident

Most close calls were errors and miscommunication. 1983's Able Archer nearly triggered a launch.

Pre-reading — The Button, Chapter 1

"The danger is not that our leaders are evil. The danger is that the system we have built gives very little room for error — and in a crisis, error is almost guaranteed."

— William Perry & Tom Collina, The Button (2020), Ch. 1 (paraphrased for discussion)

Reading assignment

Read pages 1–18. Focus on Perry's account of the moments when he, as Secretary of Defense, nearly recommended a nuclear strike based on false information. Pay attention to: how the decision was made, who was in the room, and how much time they had.

Today's focus: On August 6 and 9, 1945, the US dropped atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki. Today we examine why leaders made that decision — and what it actually meant for the people on the ground.
Today's sequence

Essay — Choose your format: technical diagram or narrative POV
Key questions

Why did they decide to bomb?

What reasoning did Truman and his advisors give — and what does the historical record reveal about their actual priorities?

Who bore the cost?

An estimated 110,000–210,000 people were killed, most of them civilians. How do we reckon with that?

What does this mean today?

Hiroshima and Nagasaki are the only two times nuclear weapons have been used in war. They define what is at stake.

Primary source — Truman's diary, July 25, 1945

Context

Written at the Potsdam Conference, two weeks before Hiroshima. One of the most direct windows into how the decision was framed by the person who made it.

"We have discovered the most terrible bomb in the history of the world... This weapon is to be used against Japan between now and August 10th. I have told the secretary of war, Mr Stimson, to use it so that military objectives and soldiers and sailors are the target and not women and children. Even if Japs are savages, ruthless, merciless and fanatic, we as the leader of the world for the common welfare cannot drop this terrible bomb on the old capital or the new... It seems to be the most terrible thing ever discovered, but it can be made the most useful."

— Harry S. Truman, diary entry, July 25, 1945, Potsdam Conference

The Hiroshima shadows

When the atomic bomb detonated over Hiroshima, the thermal flash was so intense that people sitting near stone surfaces were vaporized — leaving only their shadows burned permanently into the stone.

Photograph by Yoshito Matsushige. A journalist's legs were included to provide context to the scene.

Taken in 1946. A Japanese man recreating the likely position of the person on the steps of the Sumitomo Bank whose shadow was burned into the stone when the atomic bomb dropped.

The Hiroshima Panels

Iri and Toshi Maruki — The Hiroshima Panels (1950–1982)

Japanese artists Iri and Toshi Maruki spent over 30 years creating 15 large-scale paintings documenting the aftermath of the bombings. The panels do not show the bomb — they show its survivors: bodies, grief, and the struggle to continue living.

🔗View the Hiroshima PanelsMaruki Gallery → marukigallery.jp

Key questions

What does AI optimize for?

AI systems optimize for the goals they're given. In military war games, what happens when "winning" is the objective?

Speed vs. judgment

Nuclear decisions already happen under extreme time pressure. What changes when AI is in the loop?

The data problem

AI systems learn from historical data. But nuclear war has only happened twice — and never in the modern geopolitical context. What does this mean for AI reliability?

"We've been living under the threat of nuclear war for over 80 years. After the bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki in 1945, people in the United States grappled with a lot of uncertainty and fear. Much of the art from the 1940s–1960s turned toward abstraction — using distorted forms, surreal imagery, and biomorphic shapes to express emotions that were difficult to put into words."

Artwork 1 of 3 — look first, read context after
Gabor Peterdi, Nervous Lobster, 1947. Oil on canvas, 34 × 44½"
Before you read anything: What do you see? What do you feel? What questions come up?
✎

Your initial reaction

Gabor Peterdi was a Hungarian-American artist who studied in Italy before immigrating to the US in 1939. He enlisted in the US Army, fought in Europe during WWII, then established the Graphic Workshop at the Brooklyn Museum Art School. This piece was created using etching and gaufrage (embossing on metal plates) on zinc.

Possible interpretations
  • The distorted lobster as a symbol of nature's fragility under technological pressure
  • The mechanical quality reflects unease about technology and nature becoming entangled
  • Red coloring signals anxiety about the increasing influence of technology on society
Artwork 2 of 3 — look first, read context after
Dorothy Dehner, Woman #2, 1954. Watercolor and ink on paper, 22¾ × 18"
Look first — what do you see? What feelings or questions come up before you read anything?
✎

Your initial reaction

Dorothy Dehner was an American painter and sculptor born in 1901 in Cleveland, Ohio. After her divorce in 1950 she became more professionally active and studied printmaking at Hayter's Atelier 17. Her work focused on individuals' personal stories in the Atomic Age.

Possible interpretations
  • Meant to express internal psychological impact rather than external threat
  • A human-centered response to the Atomic Age — survival and identity, not politics
  • Fragmented, looping forms may represent tension between individual identity and social instability
Artwork 3 of 3 — look first, read context after
László Moholy-Nagy, Nuclear II, 1946. Oil on canvas (dimensions to be confirmed)
Look first — what do the shapes, colors, or composition make you feel?
✎

Your initial reaction

László Moholy-Nagy was a Hungarian painter and photographer, heavily influenced by constructivism. A strong advocate for integrating technology into the arts, he made this painting in response to Hiroshima and Nagasaki while recovering from cancer.

Possible interpretations
  • A more structured, possibly optimistic reading of nuclear energy as progress
  • White squares and triangles suggest missile-like shapes beneath the geometry
  • Vibrant colors may celebrate nuclear technology — but raises: can we separate nuclear power from nuclear weapons?

Micro-discussion facilitation
Pedagogy

The new micro-discussion format replaces long open-ended prompts with short, 1–2 sentence questions. Students write first (2–3 min), then share with a partner (2 min), then optionally share with the class.

Why this works: Students who feel under-prepared (Jason-type) can write something concrete before speaking. Students who want depth (Maya-type) can go further in their written response. Neither is forced into the other's mode.

Skeptic prompts: The orange "If you're feeling skeptical" boxes are opt-in. Don't draw attention to them unless a student seems disengaged — let students find them on their own.
